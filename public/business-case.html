02 · The honest bit

Why caring for wellbeing is hard

Most workplace wellbeing measures and programs won’t tell you caring for wellbeing is challenging. We will. Three things that quietly undermine even the most well-intentioned programs:

Chasing perfect scores is unrealistic and unhealthy.

It is healthy for people’s levels of wellbeing to ebb and flow as they navigate the inevitable ups and downs of work and life. Rather than chasing ever-higher levels of wellbeing, your goal should be helping your people improve their ability, motivation, and sense of psychological safety to actively and mindfully care for their wellbeing — even during times of challenge.

How are you measuring success?

Stress and struggle are not signs people are breaking or broken.

Feelings of struggle and stress don’t have to undermine people’s performance — if they know how to respond to these signs as opportunities for learning, growth, and resilience. Normalising struggle frees people to talk more openly and honestly about the wellbeing challenges they’re facing, and to improve their ability, motivation, and psychological safety.

What messages are you sending about struggle?

Caring for wellbeing is not a solo adventure.

Wellbeing beliefs, expectations, and behaviours spread through a complicated web of social connections at the “me”, “we”, and “us” levels. Building a thriving workplace or community environment needs informed, diverse, and sustained support to help people take tiny — but mighty — daily actions to care for their wellbeing.

03 · The framework

What does the science suggest?

We once asked Professor Martin Seligman, one of the world’s leading wellbeing experts, what would be the best way to care for wellbeing in workplaces. He said: “Measure it. Then people take action into their own hands.” But what should we be measuring? Studies show that wellbeing is not a one-dimensional concept. There is not one number you can point at, or workplace dial, several factors together build a reliable picture. One way to understand, measure, and act on evidence-based approaches for caring for wellbeing is by drawing on Professor Seligman’s PERMAH Framework, which points to six factors of feeling good and functioning effectively.

P
Positive Emotions
Boost resilience by lowering stress and mindfully navigating emotions, even when overwhelmed.
E
Engagement
Improve confidence and creativity by developing people’s neurological strengths, without overplaying them.
R
Relationships
Fuel psychological safety by creating caring & stable connections, even if dealing with difficult people.
M
Meaning
Make a sustainable positive difference for others — without burning out from painful fatigue.
A
Accomplishment
Build grit and determination to keep starting and growing, so self-doubt doesn’t keep you stuck.
H
Health
Maximise energy by eating, moving, and recovering wisely, even when life feels busy.

Depending on our values and goals, different numbers and profiles are best for different people. What matters is the ability to easily gather and understand this dashboard of wellbeing data and continue learning and prioritising the next actions.

06 · Compliance & care

Can the survey assess psychosocial risks?

A growing list of countries are advocating — and in some cases, such as Australia, regulating — for workplaces to measure and minimise psychosocial (mental and social) hazards related to people’s work. To help we have two optional sets of questions and done-for-you reports that workplaces can add to the PERMA+ Wellbeing Survey if needed.

Psychosocial risk assessment

  • Work design — lack of role clarity, unachievable job demands, low job control, inadequate reward and recognition.
  • Social support — lack of supervisor support, poor workplace relationships, bullying, harassment.
  • Work experiences — poor change management, poor physical environment, isolated and remote work.
  • Work conditions — poor organisational justice, violent and aggressive behaviours, traumatic events.

Frequency and effectiveness data is reported back to leaders for the teams for each hazard and the levels of psychological safety in the individual (“Me”), leader and team (“We”), and workplace (“Us”).

Our culture of safety & care

  • Connection — do people feel they belong here?
  • Inclusion — are differences welcomed and respected?
  • Uniqueness — are people supported to bring their authentic strengths?
  • Compassion, Appreciation, Responsibility, Emotional wisdom — the four practical actions that bring a culture of care to life across “Me”, “We”, and “Us”.

After all, while safety is a workplace requirement, studies continue to find that care is a business imperative.
