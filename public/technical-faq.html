01 · Data handling & hosting

What data, where, for how long.

1

What types of data does the application store or process?

Short version: respondent identity, response answers, and audit / operational logs — nothing more. No payment data is ever collected by the application.

Across every edition the platform stores and processes the following:

  • Personal information — respondent first name, last name, email address, role responsibility, and the demographic responses your edition has enabled (e.g. gender, age band, work status, year level for school editions). Respondent records are stored under an unguessable response key. Personal data is held on a separate database table from response answers, in line with our Privacy Policy.
  • Workplace / organisational context — whatever segmentation your contract enables (e.g. line of business, team, contract, location, year level, tenure). Used for cohort cuts in the dashboards and to allow leaders to filter their own group.
  • Survey response data — the items active in your edition’s configured sections. The evaluation can include any combination of: wellbeing factors inspired by PERMA(H), Civility, Culture of Care, Psychosocial Hazards, Economic Security, Spirituality, Moral Injury, Quietly Cracking, Digital Wellbeing (Kids / Youth), Ikigai, Health, Hope, Self-Compassion, Workplace Outcomes, plus optional free-text fields. Free-text fields are never mandatory and remain inside the same JSON payload as the rest of the response.
  • Audit + operational data — sign-in events, password reset events, administrator actions, error log entries, activity log entries, generated PDF exports, and any uploaded media (e.g. organisation logo, brand assets supplied for theming).
  • No payment data is collected by the application. Billing for the subscription is handled outside the application via Zoho Books; the platform never sees credit-card or bank-account details.
2

Where is the data hosted?

Short version: production data is hosted at the SiteGround Tier-1 data centre closest to your country, with optional residency lock to a single region.

Production hosting is on SiteGround Hosting Tier-1 data centres. Your data sits at the location closest to your country — the available regions are Sydney (Australia), Singapore, Iowa (USA), London (UK), Frankfurt (Germany), Eemshaven (Netherlands) — matching the residency map published in our Privacy Policy.

For deployments that require data not to leave a single jurisdiction (e.g. an Australian government-adjacent contract or an EU-only public-sector contract), residency can be locked to a single region under your contract. The locked region is documented in your Statement of Service and enforced at provisioning time. Each SiteGround facility provides 24×7 human security, biometric access control, and video surveillance.

3

Where are data backups stored?

Short version: backups stay in the same region as production, retained 14 days, web-inaccessible, plus the platform’s own snapshot policy on top.
  • Region: same region as production — backups never leave the contracted hosting region.
  • Mechanism: nightly logical backups (mysqldump) are written to a non-public directory on the production host (data/_backups/), gzipped, retained for 14 days, and protected from web access via .htaccess deny rules and out-of-webroot placement.
  • Platform snapshots: SiteGround additionally maintains its own platform-level snapshot policy on each facility.
02 · Access control & identity

Authentication, roles, and MFA.

1

How do users authenticate to the application?

Short version: administrators sign in with email + password (bcrypt-hashed, never stored in plain text); respondents use a single-use access link delivered by email.
  • Administrators (your wellbeing leads, HR / safety / programme owners) authenticate using email + password. Passwords are hashed using PHP’s password_hash() with bcrypt; plain-text passwords are never stored or logged.
  • Respondents authenticate via a single-use access link delivered by email (32-byte URL-safe random token). Clicking the link resolves the respondent’s identity without a password — the standard pattern for one-shot survey participation, and the only pattern that works for offline / kiosk modes used in the field.
  • Password recovery is token-based, single-use, and time-limited (60-minute expiry).
  • SSO with Azure AD / Entra ID, Okta, or Google Workspace is available and can be configured at deployment time — see 2.2.
2

Does the application support enterprise identity providers (Azure AD / Entra ID, Okta, Google Workspace)?

Short version: yes — SAML 2.0 and OIDC, with group-claim mapping for role assignment.

Yes. SAML 2.0 and OIDC are supported. SSO is configured against your tenant for the administrator surface (the dashboard side). Group-claim mapping is supported so security groups in your IdP drive administrator / co-administrator role assignment automatically — no per-user role provisioning needed.

The respondent surface remains link-based by default. Respondents in the field don’t always carry corporate credentials on personal devices, and forcing IdP authentication on the survey itself would block the offline-capable Card-mode use case for sites without reliable connectivity. SSO on the respondent side can be enabled if your contract requires it.

3

Is access restricted by role? (admin / standard / read-only)

Short version: four role tiers, all enforced server-side, every query scoped by tenant and role.

Yes. The platform implements four role tiers, all enforced server-side:

  • Platform super admin — The Good Foundry support engineers only. Cross-tenant access for support and incident response. Gated by an explicit allowlist; not visible to or grantable by the client.
  • Administrator — full access to your dashboard, configuration, respondent data, and exports. Cannot see any other client’s data.
  • Co-administrator — same data view as administrator; cannot manage other admins.
  • Respondent — sees their own results in the personal vault only. Cannot see any colleague’s data, cannot see cohort reports, cannot see any other client’s data.

Every data query is scoped server-side by subscription_id / org_id against the role on the active session. Role checks are enforced in the data layer, not just the UI — hiding a button is never the security boundary.

4

Does the application enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)?

Short version: TOTP-based MFA is available; if you use SSO, MFA enforcement is delegated to your IdP’s policies.
  • Default email + password sign-in: MFA is not enforced on the default sign-in path out of the box. Sign-in uses bcrypt-hashed passwords and session tokens.
  • TOTP MFA (Google Authenticator / Microsoft Authenticator / Authy compatible) can be enabled per-tenant for administrators as part of your deployment configuration. Enforce-or-warn behaviour, grace period, and recovery-code policy are all configurable.
  • SSO deployments: MFA enforcement is fully delegated to your IdP. Whatever MFA your IdP requires of users to sign in to other corporate apps applies automatically.
03 · Security controls & practices

Encryption, patching, scanning, testing.

1

Is data encrypted at rest?

Short version: yes — AES-256 at the file-system level, plus application-level hashing for credentials and tokens.

Yes. Hosting facilities encrypt disk volumes at the file-system level using AES-256. Database files, backup archives, and uploaded media all sit on encrypted volumes. Application-level: passwords are bcrypt-hashed; auth + reset tokens are stored as one-way hashes; session tokens are random 32-byte values.

2

Is data encrypted in transit?

Short version: yes — HTTPS only, TLS 1.2 minimum, HTTP redirected at the edge.

Yes. All traffic to and from the application is HTTPS only with TLS 1.2 minimum. Plain HTTP requests are 301-redirected to HTTPS at the edge. SSL certificates are managed via Let’s Encrypt with auto-renewal. Internal service-to-service traffic (PHP ↔ MySQL, backup write, internal log fan-out) uses the hosting provider’s private VLAN inside the contracted region.

3

How frequently are security patches applied?

Short version: application patches deploy continuously; OS / database patches are applied by the hosting provider within 72 hours of vendor publication, faster for critical CVEs.
  • Application code (PHP / JS / SQL): patched on the active deployment cycle — typically multiple times per week during normal operation, immediately for any reported security issue. Critical security patches deploy within 24 hours of identification. Deployments run via a GitHub Actions pipeline with deploy-version stamping for traceability (every deploy carries a SHA the platform can surface for verification).
  • Operating system, web server, PHP runtime, MySQL: patched by the hosting provider under their managed-hosting model. High-severity vulnerability patches are applied within 72 hours of vendor publication, with platform-wide auto-patching for critical CVEs.
4

How frequently do you perform vulnerability scanning?

Short version: continuous static + dependency scanning on every change; quarterly external application scans; ad-hoc after major architectural change.
  • Static code analysis + dependency vulnerability scanning: continuous, on every pull request — GitHub CodeQL plus Dependabot. Alerts trigger immediately on any CVE published against a dependency in use.
  • Hosting layer: the hosting provider runs continuous platform-level vulnerability scanning across each facility.
  • External application scanning: quarterly against production, plus an ad-hoc scan after any major architectural change.
5

How frequently do you perform penetration testing?

Short version: annual independent third-party pen test, plus an interim spot-test after any major architectural change.
  • Cadence: annually, with an interim spot-test after any major architectural change to the auth flow, the data schema, or sub-processor scope.
  • Engagement model: independent third-party testing across OWASP Top 10, broken access control / IDOR, injection, auth + session, and tenant-isolation verticals.
  • Reporting: a redacted summary letter from the most recent test is available to your InfoSec team under mutual NDA.
04 · Incident management & breach notification

What happens if something goes wrong.

1

Do you have a documented security incident response process?

Short version: yes — documented, tested at least annually, covers triage through post-incident review.

Yes. The Good Foundry maintains a documented Security Incident Response Plan covering: triage, severity classification (P1–P4), containment, evidence preservation, customer notification, root-cause analysis, and post-incident review. The plan is reviewed and tested at least annually.

Detection inputs include:

  • The application’s structured error_log table — every error captures timestamp, level, source, message, context (stack trace + request body), request id, user email, IP, user agent.
  • The activity_log table — every administrator action against a client record (entity type, action, detail, actor, request id, timestamp).
  • Hosting-provider host-level logs.
  • Per-server access logs ("Log files maintained for each server access" — per the Privacy Policy).
2

Are customers notified of security incidents or data breaches that may impact their data?

Short version: yes — direct email + phone call to your nominated administrator, with a written incident summary, regardless of mandatory-notification thresholds.

Yes. Where a confirmed security incident or data breach impacts your data, your nominated administrator(s) on record will be notified directly via email and follow-up phone call, with a written incident summary describing scope, root cause, impact, remediation actions taken, and forward-looking changes. Notice is sent regardless of whether the incident reaches the threshold for mandatory notification under the Australian Privacy Act’s Notifiable Data Breaches scheme, the GDPR (EU), or your local equivalent.

3

What is your breach notification timeframe?

Short version: initial notice within 72 hours; detailed report within 30 days; weekly updates while an incident is open.
  • Initial notice to you: within 72 hours of confirmed breach, in line with the Australian Privacy Act’s Notifiable Data Breaches scheme (and faster than the GDPR 72-hour rule for EU clients).
  • Detailed report: within 30 days of initial notice, including root-cause analysis and remediation summary.
  • Ongoing updates: at least weekly during an active incident until closure.

06 · Vendor access & support

When (and only when) we touch your data.

1

Do vendor staff have access to customer production data?

Short version: only for support requests you raise, implementation, or active incident response. Never for marketing, analytics, or AI model training.

Limited — only the following circumstances apply:

  • Customer-initiated support requests where a TGF support engineer needs to inspect data to diagnose a reported issue. Access is logged, time-bounded, and minimised to the smallest data set required to resolve the request.
  • Implementation engagements during go-live, where a TGF implementation lead provisions your account, theme, contract list, organisational segmentation, and admin users on your behalf.
  • Critical incident response where an active incident requires direct database inspection.

In no other circumstances does TGF staff access your production data. Your response data is never used for marketing, analytics, AI model training, or any purpose outside delivering the contracted service. Your data is never sent to a third-party AI service for training purposes — if any optional AI add-on is contracted (e.g. AI Seeding, Personalised Coaching Videos), the scope and data flow is documented separately and explicitly opt-in.

2

Is vendor access logged and auditable?

Short version: yes — every action lands in activity_log, every sign-in in auth_sessions, errors in error_log, plus the host-level access log. Retained 12 months minimum.

Yes. Every administrator action against a customer record writes to:

  • activity_log — structured per-entity audit trail (entity_type, entity_id, action, detail, user_email, request_id, timestamp).
  • auth_sessions — every sign-in event with email, IP, user agent, request id, timestamp.
  • error_log — admin-triggered actions that fail, for forensic reconstruction.
  • Web-server access log — every HTTP request, retained per the hosting provider’s policy.

Log records are retained for 12 months minimum and can be exported on request.

3

Can customer data be deleted or returned upon contract termination?

Short version: yes — full export, full deletion, or hybrid, at no cost. Backup-tape rotation completes within 35 days of a deletion request; a deletion certificate is provided.

Yes. On contract termination you may request, at no cost:

  • Full data return — a complete export of your data in machine-readable JSON / CSV (responses, contacts, configurations, logs).
  • Full data deletion — irreversible removal of all your data from production databases, backups, and any sub-processor locations storing it. Backup-tape rotation completes within 35 days of the deletion request, after which deletion is mathematically irreversible. A deletion certificate is provided on completion.
  • Hybrid — some data returned, residual data deleted, per your instruction.

The mechanism, timeline, and data-return format are documented in the Master Services Agreement / DPA before contract execution.
