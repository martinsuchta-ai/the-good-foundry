The Science Behind The Survey

Backed by Science

The PERMAH Wellbeing Survey is grounded in two decades of empirical research on positive psychology, applied psychometrics and the measurement of human flourishing. Every question, every score and every comparator on this platform traces back to a published, peer-reviewed body of work.

This page summarises the scientific foundations of the framework, the validation work behind the survey instrument, and the ongoing research that continues to evolve PERMA+ for use in workplaces, schools and communities worldwide.

Foundations

The PERMA Theory of Wellbeing

Developed by Dr. Martin E.P. Seligman at the Penn Positive Psychology Center, PERMA proposes that human flourishing is supported by five measurable elements: Positive Emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning and Accomplishment.

PERMA shifted the field from a deficit-based view of wellbeing (the absence of distress) to a strengths-based one: flourishing has positive properties that can be measured, supported and grown.

Why it matters

Each PERMA element predicts independent variance in life satisfaction and is responsive to evidence-based intervention — making the model both a measurement framework and a practical map for change.

From PERMA to PERMAH

Subsequent research demonstrated that physical Health — sleep, movement, vitality — is not merely a correlate of flourishing but an integral pillar of it. Adding the +H to PERMA produced PERMAH: a six-element model that captures the body as well as the mind.

The PERMAH Wellbeing Survey was conceived by Dr. Michelle McQuaid and developed in partnership with Professor Peggy Kern (University of Melbourne) and original contributor Martin Suchta to translate PERMAH into a validated, applied measurement instrument.

What we measure

The survey scores each element on a 0–100 scale, lets respondents see themselves against population norms, and surfaces the amplifiers — ability, motivation and psychological safety — that turn intent into behaviour.

Psychometric Foundations

Each PERMAH item passed through iterative scale-development work led by Professor Peggy Kern: factor structure, internal consistency, test–retest reliability, and convergent / discriminant validity against established measures of life satisfaction, depression, anxiety and engagement.

Scoring is normalised across populations and across editions so a respondent in a healthcare team in Melbourne is comparable to a respondent in a manufacturing team in Glasgow.

Key properties

  • Multi-element factor structure validated in working adults, students and clinical samples
  • Reliable population norms across age, role and tenure cohorts
  • Sensitivity to change — scores shift in response to genuine intervention, not only to repeat exposure

Workplace Applications

Workforce datasets from organisations across healthcare, education, professional services, manufacturing and the public sector show consistent links between PERMAH scores and the outcomes leaders care about — engagement, retention, psychological safety, sick leave, and team performance.

Population averages, role-band breakdowns and time-series trends are continuously refreshed so the comparators on every dashboard reflect the current state of the field rather than a frozen snapshot.

What this enables

Leaders see not just a score, but a score in context: how their workforce sits against benchmark, where the leverage points are, and which interventions a similar population has responded to.

The PERMA+ Extension

Beyond the PERMAH foundation, ongoing research has produced PERMA+: a methodological extension that integrates additional dimensions of wellbeing emerging from contemporary research, longitudinal survey data, and applied practice across workplaces, schools, and communities.

PERMA+ adds explicit measurement of Psychosocial Hazards (developed by Dr. Michelle McQuaid) and Culture of Care (developed by Dr. McQuaid) so the framework reaches beyond individual flourishing to the systemic and cultural conditions that enable wellbeing at scale. Research and evaluation expertise is contributed by Dr. Stewart I. Donaldson and Dr. Scott Donaldson, with Danielle Jacobs leading the practitioner translation of the framework into client engagements. Continued scientific development is led by Martin Suchta, Director of The Good Foundry.

PERMA+ is offered as a research framework and methodological evolution, building on — and acknowledging — Dr. Seligman’s foundational PERMA theory of flourishing.

Research, Validity & Reliability

How we know the PERMAH Tool works

The PERMAH Tool is a measurement instrument that provides insights into the wellbeing of individuals and teams within the context of schools, organisations, and communities. Based on Dr. Martin Seligman’s PERMA Model (2011), it measures wellbeing across six factors — Positive Emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment and Health — at the individual, team, and workplace level.

Measuring wellbeing is complex. Studies have shown that most people maintain relatively healthy and stable wellbeing over time even in the face of challenge (Bonanno, 2004; Diener, Lucas & Scollon, 2006; Capic, Li & Cummins, 2014); that wellbeing perceptions, experiences and behaviours are diverse and spread through complex social networks (Christakis & Fowler, 2009; Jarden, 2016); and that any self-report instrument carries inherent biases and limitations (Donaldson & Grant-Vallone, 2002).

Responsible scientific research never claims to be “proven” — it is always testing what we think we know to keep the field learning (Kern et al., 2019). With those challenges always in mind, we are committed to ongoing transparency about the validity and reliability of the science behind the PERMAH Tool. Four points guide us: theoretical grounding, psychometric validity, consistency, and continued research.

The Four Points

1. Theoretical Grounding

The value of measurement is often visible through the numbers it produces — but equally important is the theory those measures are built on. The PERMAH Tool is grounded in the wellbeing theory originally proposed by Dr. Martin Seligman (2011), drawing on more than twenty years of research.

Seligman identified five essential elements that contribute to wellbeing: Positive Emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning and Accomplishment. We — and many others, including the World Health Organisation — consider physical health an integral pillar of wellbeing too. With Dr. Seligman’s permission we add the “H” for Health and refer to the model as PERMAH.

Just as we describe the construct of weather using elements such as temperature, humidity, wind speed and barometric pressure, PERMAH attempts to describe wellbeing through its component elements (Seligman, 2018). Each element is included because it: independently leads to wellbeing; can be pursued for its own intrinsic value; and can be defined and measured independently of all others.

The PERMA Profiler & co-creation

Dr. Peggy Kern — one of the PERMAH Tool co-creators — and her colleagues developed The PERMA Profiler (Butler & Kern, 2016) to measure each PERMA element (3 items per element, 15 items total) plus physical health, negative emotions, loneliness and happiness. The Profiler has been translated into Turkish (Aye, 2018), Japanese (Watanabe et al., 2018), Greek (Pezirkianidis et al., 2019), Mandarin (Kern et al., 2018; Lai et al., 2018) and German (Wammerl et al., 2019), and has been used to compare wellbeing across countries (Iasiello et al., 2017; Khaw & Kern, 2015), to measure staff wellbeing (Kern et al., 2014; Lovett & Lovett, 2016), and at the employee, team and supervisor levels (Donaldson, Donaldson, McQuaid & Kern, 2024).

Empirical work has continued (Donaldson et al., 2023; 2024; Donaldson, 2019; Donaldson, Lee & Donaldson, 2019; Heshmati et al., 2020; Kern et al., 2014; Watanabe et al., 2018). Some studies surface a five-element factor structure; others recover a three-factor structure of Positive Emotion, Relationships–Meaning, and Accomplishment–Engagement (Khaw & Kern, 2015), or only two factors (Umucu et al., 2019; Ryan et al., 2019). Goodman et al. (2018) found PERMA strongly associated with subjective wellbeing.

Dr. Seligman (2018) has argued the data strongly supports the theory: PERMA captures wellbeing and identifies at least some of its building blocks. Bartholomaeus et al. (2021) and Pezirkianidis et al. (2019) replicated the five-factor structure while also finding a general wellbeing factor — an important aspect of the model.

We have not chosen PERMA because we believe it is “proven” — good science is never proven, only continually refined. We have chosen it because Seligman’s assertion that PERMA gives people a useful, accessible way to understand, measure and act on wellbeing using evidence-based tools holds true. A meta-analysis of positive psychology workplace interventions (Donaldson, Lee & Donaldson, 2019) found that PERMA-focused interventions had a positive effect on both work outcomes and wellbeing precisely because of their multidimensional approach.

2. Psychometric Validity

Measuring psychological states is challenging — especially when matching a tool to a population and context. The most common question is the right one: does this instrument measure what it is designed to measure? Answering it well requires ongoing, reflective measurement of validity and reliability. That is our commitment.

The PERMAH Tool was originally based on the PERMA Profiler items. As organisations asked us to gather measures at different times and at the organisational level, we worked with Dr. Kern to add new items at those scales. We also partnered with Dr. Richard Ryan to incorporate validated measures from self-determination theory.

Latest psychometric results (July 2021)

We test the psychometric validity of the PERMAH Tool on a regular basis. The most recent results show strong scale reliability and internal consistency for individual factors, with the full instrument also performing strongly:

Cronbach’s alpha values above 0.8 indicate good internal consistency at the factor level; the whole-instrument alpha of 0.97 indicates the tool measures a coherent construct.

3a. Consistency Over Time

Wellbeing is an ongoing journey, not a one-time destination. We look at the consistency of responses and the stability of relationships within the data over time. The reliability of the PERMAH Tool is amplified when it is used to map wellbeing across multiple time points rather than once.

Many of our clients have welcomed mapping wellbeing over time precisely because it reveals insights that would otherwise stay hidden — including the surprising truth that wellbeing comprises both thriving and struggle, and how the three amplifiers (ability, motivation and psychological safety) carry intent into behaviour.

3b. Consistency Across Populations

Alongside the data collected through the tool itself, we periodically invest in randomised, nationally representative survey data in Australia, Canada, the United States and a growing list of other countries to understand population-level patterns.

This data is published in freely available reports and added as population norms in the survey for comparison. The breadth of data continually validates our measures and provides representative comparators directly inside the tool.

4. A Commitment To Continued Research

The research landscape is dynamic and constantly evolving. The tool continues to carefully and thoughtfully consider the fundamental building blocks on which the evidence rests — the generation of questions, the theoretical connections, validity and reliability, and the co-creation with experts.

We have assembled a research review board of leading wellbeing, change, leadership and diversity researchers around the world, alongside independent analysts, to regularly assess the measures we use, review the data we gather, and surface the insights we are gaining. This serves a two-fold purpose: it deepens our understanding of relationships and trends in the data, and it acts as a safeguard — the analysts flag any improbable relationships that could indicate survey miscalibration. So far, that has not happened.

In The End

We once asked Prof. Seligman — one of the world’s leading wellbeing experts — what would be the best way to care for wellbeing in schools, workplaces and communities. He said:

“Measure it. Then people will take action into their own hands.” — Prof. Martin E.P. Seligman

As researchers, we tend to measure what we treasure, and for us, wellbeing is what we treasure. Our aim is to help people think more rigorously about research and measurement — and to recognise that while reliability and validity are important, they are not the only factors that matter when choosing a wellbeing instrument.

By being open and transparent about our theoretical grounding, psychometric validity, consistency and continued research, we hope you feel informed and confident that the PERMAH Tool is a valid and reliable instrument that will offer valuable insights into a system’s wellbeing.
